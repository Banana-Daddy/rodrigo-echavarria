The Story
From Bogotá to the Garden State, by way of every clay court in between.

There’s a particular kind of player who survives the lower tiers of professional tennis — the ITF World Tennis Tour, the Challengers, the qualifying rounds — and walks away not with a Grand Slam but with something arguably more useful: a 360-degree understanding of how the sport actually works. Rodrigo Echavarría is one of those players.

Born in Colombia in 1996, right-handed, a clay-court specialist by heritage and by habit, he turned pro in 2018, climbed the international ranking ladder for four years, and posted a 49–44 career singles record across hard, clay, and indoor surfaces. He held the #1 ranking in Colombia. He sparred with the country’s national champions. Then, with the same deliberation he’d brought to a backhand down the line, he chose to coach.

Eight years in, he’s the Director of Tournament Training at Garden State Tennis Center in Edison, New Jersey — a club whose teaching staff has produced over fifty ETA #1 juniors, three NCAA national champions, and players who have competed at the US Open, Wimbledon, and Roland Garros.

His lessons run $110 an hour. He works with top-ranked juniors, college hopefuls, and adults serious enough to drive across two counties. He prefers clay. He speaks fluent Spanish. He doesn’t advertise.

This is how a former pro builds the next ones.
